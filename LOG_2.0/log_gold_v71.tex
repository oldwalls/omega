\section{The LOG Principle: A New Starting Point}
\label{sec:principle}

\subsection{Motivation: Why Optimisation?}

Physical law is often written as a conservation or
an extremum. Newton's second law is a conservation of momentum.
Maxwell's equations follow from the extremum of an
electromagnetic action. General relativity follows from
extremising the Einstein-Hilbert action.
The Principle of Least Action~\cite{Feynman} does not
derive every number in physics --- it does not tell you
the electron mass or the value of $\alpha$ --- but it
provides the \emph{shape} in which laws must appear.

The LOG principle asks a different question.
Not ``what is extremised?'' but ``what configuration
achieves the greatest ratio of new prediction to
thermodynamic cost?''

Formally: among all configurations of a physical system,
the ones that persist are those maximising
\emph{predictive information per unit entropy expenditure.}

This is not a new claim about quantum mechanics or
field theory. It is a new claim about \emph{selection}:
given a space of possible spectral configurations,
the LOG principle filters it to the configurations
that are maximally informative relative to their cost.

\subsection{The Omega Functional}
\label{sec:omega}

\begin{definition}[LOG Omega functional~\cite{LogOmega}]
For a physical process $P$ operating over time interval $[0,\tau]$:
\begin{equation}
  \Om[P]
  \;:=\;
  \frac{\Delta I_{\mathrm{pred}}[P;\,0,\tau]}
       {\Sigma[P] + \varepsilon},
\label{eq:omega}
\end{equation}
where $\Delta I_{\mathrm{pred}}$ is the gain in predictive
information (reduction in forecast uncertainty), $\Sigma[P]$
is total entropy production, and $\varepsilon > 0$ is a
regularisation ensuring the functional is finite.
\end{definition}

The functional $\Om[P]$ is dimensionless.
Maximising it selects processes that are \emph{efficient
predictors}: they extract the most inferential leverage
per unit of irreversibility.

\subsection{What Is a Spectrum?}

A compact Riemannian manifold $\mathcal{M}$ --- a closed,
bounded, smoothly curved space --- has a natural
differential operator called the \emph{Laplacian} $\Delta$.
On a circle of radius $R$, the Laplacian acts on
functions $f(\theta)$ as $-d^2f/d\theta^2$.
On a 2-sphere, it is the familiar angular part of the
Laplacian in spherical coordinates.

The Laplacian has a discrete set of
\emph{eigenvalues} $\lambda_n$ and
corresponding \emph{eigenfunctions} $\phi_n$:
\begin{equation}
  -\Delta\,\phi_n \;=\; \lambda_n\,\phi_n.
\end{equation}
These eigenvalues are the \emph{natural frequencies}
of the manifold.
The collection $\{\lambda_n\}$ is its \emph{spectrum}.
Just as a violin string has specific resonant frequencies
determined by its length and tension, a manifold has
specific eigenvalues determined by its shape and curvature.
The spectrum encodes geometric information
about the manifold in a purely algebraic object.

\subsection{Why the Vector Laplacian?}

We study the \emph{vector Laplacian} --- the Laplacian
acting on 1-forms (vector fields) rather than scalar
functions. This is the physically appropriate operator
for a U(1) gauge field, because electromagnetic
potentials $A_\mu$ are 1-forms.

The key distinction: the \emph{scalar} Laplacian on $S^4$
counts scalar modes (spin-0).
The \emph{vector} Laplacian counts the
\emph{transverse vector modes} (the physical degrees of
freedom of a gauge field after removing longitudinal
and ghost contributions via Hodge decomposition).
Using the wrong Laplacian gives the wrong spectrum
and does not produce $\alpha^{-1} = 137.036$ at any radius.
This is verified explicitly in the ingredient audit
(Section~\ref{sec:audit}).

\subsection{What Is a Heat Kernel?}

The \emph{heat kernel} of an operator $\Delta$ is the
solution to the heat equation
$(\partial_t + \Delta)K = 0$ with $K(x,x,0) = \delta(x-x)$.
Spectrally, it is:
\begin{equation}
  K(x,y,t) \;=\; \sum_n e^{-\lambda_n t}\,\phi_n(x)\phi_n(y).
\end{equation}
The trace $\mathrm{tr}\,K(t) = \sum_n e^{-\lambda_n t}$
is a generating function for the spectrum.
At small $t$, it encodes short-distance geometry
(Weyl's law).
At large $t$, it is dominated by the lowest eigenvalue.

In our functional, the factor $e^{-x_n}$ in
$S(R)$ is precisely the heat kernel evaluated at
$t = 1/R^2$ --- the Planck time at scale $R$.
The heat kernel suppression ensures that modes with
$\lambda_n R^2 > 1$ (sub-Planck modes) are
exponentially suppressed.
This is not an ad hoc cutoff; it is the natural
Planck-scale regularisation provided by heat-kernel
methods in quantum field theory on curved spaces.

\subsection{What Is Fisher Information?}

Given a probability distribution $p(x;\theta)$
depending on a parameter $\theta$,
the \emph{Fisher information} is:
\begin{equation}
  \mathcal{I}(\theta)
  \;=\;
  \mathbb{E}\!\left[\left(\frac{\partial \log p}{\partial\theta}\right)^2\right].
\end{equation}
It measures how much the distribution changes
when $\theta$ changes --- equivalently,
how much inferential leverage an observation gives
about $\theta$.

In the context of a Gaussian path integral over
field modes, the Fisher information of a mode
with eigenvalue $\lambda$ is proportional to $1/\lambda^2$:
high-frequency modes (large $\lambda$) carry less
information per mode than low-frequency modes.
The $1/x_n^2$ factor in $S(R)$ is this Fisher
information density.

The $\pi/4$ prefactor in $S(R)$ is the Gaussian
Fisher normalisation of the two-sector Hodge decomposition
on $S^4$ (transverse gauge + longitudinal/ghost).
It equals $(\sqrt{\pi}/2)^2$, the product of two
Gaussian Fisher normalisation constants --- one
for each sector. This is derived in Section~\ref{sec:pi4}.

\subsection{Weyl's Law and Its Corrections}
\label{sec:weyl_intro}

For a compact $d$-dimensional manifold,
Weyl's law~\cite{Weyl} states:
\begin{equation}
  N(\lambda) \;\sim\;
  \frac{\mathrm{Vol}(\mathcal{M})}{(4\pi)^{d/2}\,\Gamma(d/2+1)}\,\lambda^{d/2}
  \quad\text{as }\lambda\to\infty,
\end{equation}
where $N(\lambda)$ counts eigenvalues up to $\lambda$.
This is the \emph{leading} term.

On a curved manifold, there are \emph{corrections} to
Weyl's law that encode the curvature.
For the vector Laplacian on $S^4$, the degeneracy
of the $n$-th eigenvalue is exactly:
\begin{equation}
  d_n \;=\; \frac{(n+1)^2(n+2)^2}{4n(n+3)},
  \quad n = 1, 2, 3, \ldots
\label{eq:dn_exact}
\end{equation}
For large $n$, this expands as:
\begin{equation}
  d_n \;=\;
  \underbrace{\frac{n^2}{4}}_{\text{Weyl leading term}}
  +\;
  \underbrace{\frac{3n}{4}}_{\text{first curvature correction}}
  +\; \frac{1}{2} + O(n^{-1}).
\label{eq:dn_expand}
\end{equation}
The $n^2/4$ term is what a flat 4-manifold would give.
The $3n/4$ term is the first signature of positive
curvature --- the sphere is not flat, and this
difference propagates into the mode counting.
As we will show in Section~\ref{sec:asymptotic},
this apparently small correction ($3n/4$ versus $n^2/4$)
is responsible for the difference between the
geometric floor $135.039$ and the observed
$\alpha^{-1} = 137.036$ --- a physically crucial $1.5\%$.

\subsection{Why $S^4$?}
\label{sec:whyS4}

The 4-sphere $S^4$ is the simplest compact, orientable,
simply-connected 4-manifold.
It is the Euclidean continuation of 4-dimensional
Minkowski spacetime when compactified at a finite scale.

Three properties make it the natural arena for
the LOG principle at Planck scale:

\begin{enumerate}
\item \textbf{Topological simplicity.}
  $H^1(S^4;\mathbb{R}) = 0$: the first de~Rham
  cohomology vanishes.
  This means every closed 1-form on $S^4$ is exact,
  and the Hodge decomposition
  $A = A_{\mathrm{T}} + d\phi$
  holds globally without harmonic obstructions.
  For a U(1) gauge field, this is precisely the
  condition that makes the path integral measure
  factorise cleanly into transverse (physical)
  and longitudinal (pure gauge) sectors.
  The $\pi/4$ Fisher normalisation follows directly
  (Section~\ref{sec:pi4}).

\item \textbf{Maximal symmetry.}
  $S^4$ has the largest isometry group ($SO(5)$)
  of any compact 4-manifold.
  This means its spectrum is completely classified:
  every eigenvalue and multiplicity is known in
  closed form (equation~\eqref{eq:dn_exact}).
  There is no ambiguity in the spectrum; every
  computation is exact.

\item \textbf{Positive curvature.}
  $S^4$ has constant positive sectional curvature $1/R^2$.
  This curvature produces the $3n/4$ correction
  in~\eqref{eq:dn_expand}, which is the source
  of the spectral excess above the geometric floor.
  A flat manifold ($\mathbb{T}^4$, $\mathbb{R}^4/\Gamma$)
  would have $d_n \approx n^2/4$ only, and would
  not reproduce $\alpha^{-1} = 137.036$.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{The Planck Cutoff}
\label{sec:cutoff}

\begin{Tbox}[Planck cutoff]
We restrict to modes with eigenvalue below the
Planck scale:
\begin{equation}
  x_n \;:=\; \frac{n(n+3)}{R^2} \;<\; 1
  \quad\Longleftrightarrow\quad
  \lambda_n < \ell_P^{-2}.
\label{eq:cutoff}
\end{equation}
Modes with $\lambda_n \geq \ell_P^{-2}$ oscillate
faster than the Planck frequency and are excluded.
This gives an upper cutoff on $n$:
\begin{equation}
  n_{\max}(R)
